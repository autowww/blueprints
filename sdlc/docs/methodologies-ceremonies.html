Ceremonies

Foundation = intent types (C1–C6) + practice suggestions. Bridge = crosswalk to methodology names. Forks = Scrum/Kanban/phased/XP + event suggestions. SDLC phases tie-in in SDLC.md §2. Canonical: ceremonies/README.md.

Spec-driven I/O: For each intent C1–C6, see preconditions, inputs, outputs, and filled examples in Ceremonies (SDD I/O) + template CEREMONY-INTENT.template.md. Process slices (refinement, planning, gate, …): Process (SDD I/O).

Foundation vs forks

  • Foundation (ceremony-foundation.md) defines why collaboration repeats—align, commit, sync, inspect, improve, assure—without Scrum/Kanban names.
  • Forks name official or common rituals and map them to C1–C6; cadence and rules stay in each methodology.
  • Roles (archetypes) say who tends to lead; Phases A–F say what docs the lifecycle expects.

Typical phase touch

Where intents most often appear across SDLC A–F—methodology may shift emphasis. Full matrix: ceremony-foundation.md.

  • C1–C2 — strong in A–B; C2 also D when pulling work.
  • C3 — D–E while building and verifying.
  • C4–C6 — E–F for inspection, quality, release.

Who leads (archetypes)

Example: C2 often co-led by Demand + Build; C5 by Flow. See foundation § Intent × archetypes and Roles & archetypes.

SDLC phases ↔ intents

SDLC.md §2 opens with a table mapping Phases A–F to which intents are usually engaged (planning, build, verify, release). Ceremonies support the phase obligations—they do not replace WBS/specs/CI docs.

Practice suggestions (foundation)

Per-intent hints: minimum viable, prefer, avoid, and doc hooks to phases and tracking. Full text: ceremony-foundation.md § Practice suggestions.

  • C1 — written goal + out-of-scope; avoid crisis-only alignment.
  • C2 — visible selected work + capacity check; avoid silent overflow.
  • C3 — blockers surfaced in 24h; avoid status-only with no escalation.
  • C4 — demo Done work; stakeholders who can accept.
  • C5 — one experiment with owner; use data not only opinions.
  • C6 — checklist + CI automation; shift left on risk.

Principles

  • Ceremony ≠ meeting—async can satisfy intent if decisions are real.
  • Git supports narratives; Sprint Goals, blockers, acceptance often need ALM or gate records.
  • One Contributor pipeline in tracking repos—see project sdlc/TRACKING-FOUNDATION.md.

Intent cycle (diagram)

How C1–C6 often repeat across a delivery horizon—actual cadence depends on methodology (Scrum sprint vs Kanban replenishment, etc.).

flowchart LR C1[C1 Align] --> C2[C2 Commit] C2 --> C3[C3 Sync] C3 --> C4[C4 Inspect] C4 --> C6[C6 Assure] C6 --> C5[C5 Improve] C5 --> C1

Not a mandated sequence every week—some intents happen continuously (e.g. C3 via pairing). See ceremony-foundation.md.
